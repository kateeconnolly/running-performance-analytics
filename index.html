Model Design

Ridge regression predicts pace (min/mile) from six training-load features. Ridge was chosen over OLS because the rolling mileage windows are correlated — the L2 penalty stabilises coefficient estimates under multicollinearity.

The train/test split is chronological, not random: the first 80% of runs (by date) form the training set, and the final 20% the test set. This ensures the model is always evaluated on runs it has never seen and that post-date all training data.

Key finding: The model returns negative R² on the held-out test set, meaning it does not outperform a naive mean predictor on the most recent 9 months of data. Diagnostics reveal a −0:40/mile shift in mean pace between training (8:47/mi) and test (8:07/mi) periods. You got measurably faster — in a way a linear trend trained on 4 prior years could not anticipate. This illustrates a known limitation of linear regression on non-stationary physiological time series.

Pipeline & Methodology

Four stages from raw Strava export to publication-quality figures, runnable end-to-end with a single command.

Step 1

Data Cleaning

Filters to running activities, converts Strava's km export to miles, parses timestamps, and removes rows outside physically plausible pace bounds (3–30 min/mile).

Step 2

Feature Engineering

Builds rolling 7, 28, and 56-day mileage windows from a daily aggregation. A shift(1) ensures only prior days are included — no data leakage. Classifies each run Easy / Moderate / Hard by pace tertile.

Step 3

Pace Modelling

Ridge regression trained on six features using an 80/20 chronological split. Features are standardised for comparable coefficients. Outputs are associative, not causal.

Step 4

Visualisation

Six publication-quality figures with Matplotlib and seaborn. Consistent colour palette and typography across all charts, exported at 150 dpi.
